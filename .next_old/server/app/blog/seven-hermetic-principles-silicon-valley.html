As Above, So Below: Why Hermeticism Resonates with Silicon Valley

Kybalion<sup class="cross-link-icon">✦</sup></a>*, published in 1908 but based on ancient Hermetic traditions, outlines seven universal principles. To the uninitiated, they sound like occult theory. To a systems engineer, they sound like architecture.

1. The Principle of Mentalism

"The All is Mind; The Universe is Mental." In the digital age, we've built a literal version of this. Our "world" is increasingly a simulation made of code—pure thought rendered into logic gates. If the universe is mental, then "hacking" the universe is simply a matter of understanding the underlying logic.

Correspondence<sup class="cross-link-icon">✦</sup></a> "As above, so below; as below, so above." This is the essence of recursive design and fractals. In systems thinking, the bugs in the micro-services often mirror the architectural flaws of the entire organization. When we fix the "micro," the "macro" souvent follows.

3. The Principle of Vibration

"Nothing rests; everything moves; everything vibrates." In Silicon Valley, we see this in frequency trading and signal processing. Everything is data in motion. Understanding the "vibration" or the "pulse" of a market is the difference between profit and obsolescence.

4. The Principle of Polarity

"Everything is dual; everything has poles." In UI/UX, we balance the tension between "Frictionless" and "Meaningful." In AI, we balance "Creativity" against "Accuracy." These are not contradictions to be solved, but poles to be navigated.

Hermeticism doesn't ask you to believe in magic; it asks you to recognize the patterns. When engineers realize that the ancient laws of Hermes match the modern laws of information theory, the "occult" becomes the "obvious."
