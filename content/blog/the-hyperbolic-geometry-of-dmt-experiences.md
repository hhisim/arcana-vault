## The Geometry of DMT States

This essay analyzes the phenomenology of DMT through algorithmic, geometric, and information-theoretic frameworks. This distinguishes it from purely phenomenological, symbolic, neuroscientific, or spiritual accounts. We do not claim to know what ultimately implements the effects here described (i.e., in light of the substrate problem of consciousness), but the analysis does not need to go there to have explanatory power.

We posit that one can account for a wide array of diverse phenomena present in DMT-induced states of consciousness by describing the overall changes in the geometry of one's spatiotemporal representations—what we call "world-sheets" (3D + time surfaces; 3D1T).

The concrete hypothesis: The network of subjective measurements of distances we experience on DMT has an overall geometry that can accurately be described as hyperbolic (or hyperbolic-like). In other words, our inner 3D1T world grows larger than is possible to fit in an experiential field with 3D Euclidean phenomenal space (i.e., an experience of dimension R2.5 representing an R3 scene). This results in phenomenal spaces, surfaces, and objects acquiring a mean negative curvature.

While DMT produces this effect most consistently and intensely, it is also present in states induced by tryptamines and, to a lesser extent, all psychedelics.

## What is Hyperbolic Geometry?

The clearest way to describe hyperbolic space is through examples. A saddle surface is a 2-dimensional hyperbolic space (also called "hyperbolic planes" or H2). For a surface to have "constant curvature," it must look the same at every point. Saddles have the property that the angles of a triangle found in them add up to less than 180 degrees (compare to surfaces with positive curvature like the 2-sphere, where angles add up to more than 180 degrees).

An important property of hyperbolic spaces is how the area of a circle (or n-dimensional volume of a hypersphere) increases as a function of its radius. In 2D Euclidean space, area grows quadratically with radius. But on H2, area grows exponentially! As you may imagine, it is easy to get lost in hyperbolic space. A few steps take you to an entirely different scene.

## The Phenomenology of DMT: The 6 Levels

Here is an account of a typical vaporized DMT experience, divided into six stages:

1. **Threshold (3-30 seconds)**: The first hint is a change in the apparent ambiance of one's setting. A feeling that something important is about to happen.

2. **The Chrysanthemum (4-8 mg)**: A surface saturated with intricate symmetrical relationships, bright colors, shifting edges, and shimmering pulsing patterns. This is often described as "the best screen saver ever"—harmonious, symmetric, and beautiful in uncountable ways.

3. **The Magic Eye Level**: The Chrysanthemum becomes the texture of an autostereogram. The world-sheet (depth maps) becomes capable of representing arbitrary situations, including entities in places like ice-cream stores and furniture rooms.

4. **Waiting Room (12-25 mg)**: The world-sheet breaks into several quasi-independent components. The total 2D surface curvature is substantially higher. One begins to see actual 3D hyperbolic space. Entities can interact directly with you.

5. **Breakthrough (20-30 mg)**: A curvature so extreme that topological bifurcations start to happen uncontrollably. Space twists and reconnects to itself in complex ways. A sense of radical openness is often experienced.

6. **Amnesia Level**: The difficulty of instantiating the necessary geometry to make sense of what was experienced leads to forgetting.

## We Can Only Remember What We Can Reconstruct

We cannot experience H2 or H3 manifolds under normal circumstances, but we can represent some aspects through partial embeddings and projections into more familiar geometries. Such representations will necessarily be flawed. It is notoriously hard to truly embed H2 in Euclidean 3D space, since doing so will necessarily distort some properties.

This difficulty is crucial for understanding why DMT experiences are so hard to remember. To remember the experience, you need to create a faithful 3D Euclidean embedding of it. If you experience a hyperbolic object and want to remember it, you must think strategically about how to fold, crunch, and deform it to fit in compact Euclidean representations.
